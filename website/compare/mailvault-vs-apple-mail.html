MailVault vs Apple Mail for Email Archiving — Which Is Better?

5 min read

Apple Mail is an excellent email client. It ships with every Mac, syncs seamlessly with iCloud, and handles day-to-day email without fuss. If your goal is reading and writing email, Apple Mail does the job well.

But Apple Mail was never designed for email archiving. If you need to back up old emails locally, free up server storage, or maintain a portable archive of important messages, its limitations become apparent quickly. That is exactly where MailVault comes in.

The Core Difference

Apple Mail is a general-purpose email client. MailVault is a dedicated email archiving tool. They solve different problems, and comparing them only makes sense when the task at hand is archiving, backup, or server cleanup.

When Apple Mail Is the Better Choice

If you primarily need an email client for daily use, Apple Mail is hard to beat on a Mac. It integrates tightly with macOS — Spotlight indexes your mail, Handoff lets you continue on your iPhone, and iCloud keeps everything in sync. Its rules, VIP contacts, and smart mailboxes make organizing incoming mail straightforward. For sending, receiving, and managing active email, Apple Mail is purpose-built and polished.

When MailVault Is the Better Choice

MailVault shines when the goal shifts from using email to preserving it. Common scenarios where MailVault is a better fit:

  • Server space cleanup: Your mailbox is full and your provider charges for more storage. Archive old emails locally, delete them from the server, and reclaim space without losing anything.
  • Compliance and retention: You need to keep emails for legal or regulatory reasons but do not want them sitting on a remote server indefinitely.
  • Portable backup: MailVault stores emails as standard .eml files. You can open them in any email client, move them to another machine, or back them up to an external drive. Apple Mail's proprietary database is not designed for portability.
  • Switching providers: If you move from one email provider to another, MailVault lets you archive everything from the old account before you close it.
  • Offline-first access: Archived emails in MailVault are always available, even without an internet connection. Apple Mail's offline access depends on what it has cached.

Storage Format Matters

Apple Mail stores emails in an internal database using the .emlx format and a supporting SQLite index. This works fine as long as you stay inside Apple Mail, but extracting individual emails for backup or migration is cumbersome. If the database becomes corrupted, recovery is difficult.

MailVault uses the Maildir format — each email is a separate .eml file on disk. This is the same standard format used by email servers worldwide. If MailVault disappeared tomorrow, your archived emails would still be readable by Thunderbird, Outlook, or any other client that supports .eml files.

Not a Replacement

MailVault is not a full replacement for Apple Mail. It does not aim to be your primary email client. It does not have calendar integration, smart mailboxes, or VIP contacts. What it does — archiving, local retention, and server cleanup — it does better than any general-purpose email client, because that is its entire purpose.

Many users run both: Apple Mail for daily email, and MailVault for periodic archiving and backup. The two complement each other well.
